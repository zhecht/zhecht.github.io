The End of Modern Cryptography?

The Short Answer? No Way

In the news, you can find numerous articles with titles similar to the likes of The beginning of the end for encryption schemes?. This is somewhat counter-intuitive though. Numerous algorithms used in the real-world will become obselete once quantum computers are introduced into our lifes. On the other hand, over enough time, a plethora of new encryption schemes will be created. So the problem we face today...do we have enough time to prepare for the inevitable.

The Mosca Theorem

Michele Mosca proposed a simple theorem with huge implications. He defines x as the time (in years) it will take for encryption to become secure, y is how long it will take to re-tool existing infrastructures with quantum safe solutions, and lastly z, how long it will take until a large-scale quantum computer is built.

If x + y > z, worry.

NIST and other experts expect that a quantum computer will be built within 15 years [1]. Some scientists even believe we are already at the point where y > z [2]. If this is the case, then we are already too late. Many security companies and intelligent angencies are well on their way to implementing post-quantum standards. These are schemes that, in theory, are unbreakable even with the access of a quantum computer.

Time For A Change

All public key crypto (RSA, DSA, Diffie-Hellman exchange) will be vulnerable to attacks. Symmetric key cryptography (AES) and Hash functions (SHA-1,2,3) would need a modification that can affect runtime and memory. Symmetric key crypto would need larger keys and hash functions would need longer outputs. Public key cryptography together with quantum-resitant algorithms can also fix this problem.

Quantum-Resistant Algorithms

  • Secret-Key Crypto (AES)
  • Multivariate-quadratic equations
  • Lattice-Based (NTRU)
  • Code-Based (McEliece)
  • Hash-Based (Merkle's Tree)
In theory, these algorithms are thought to be safe from an adversary equipped with a quantum computer. The reason public key cryptography works in practice is due to three properties. 1) The Integer Factorization Problem, 2) The Discrete Log Problem, and 3) The Elliptic-Curve Discrete Log Problem. Scientists have shown that with the assistance of a quantum computer, all these problems can be managed in feasible time. Peter Shor introduced the Shor algorithm in 1994, shocking the world of quantum computing.

Shor's Algorithm

The Shor algorithm can factor large integers in polynomial time, thus making RSA insecure. The fastest known scheme to break RSA at the time is called the number-field sieve. The number-field sieve, presented in 1988, still has a runtime similar to factorization algorithms presented today. It can factor any RSA % N using 2(1.9...+o(1))(lg N)(1/3) (lg lg N)(2/3) operations. 6 years later and Shor presented that a quantum computer of size (lg N)1+o(1) can factor RSA % N in only (lg N)2+o(1) operations.

Before we get into an example of how Shor's works, we need to clear up some layman quantum terms for the beginners (like me).

Definitions

  • Quantum Bit (Qubit): Like a bit, a qubit holds information. According to the no-cloning theorem and no-deleting theorem, a qubit's information cannot be copied nor destroyed. A qubit can exist not only in 0 and 1 states (like a bit), but also any combination of the two. **CAVEAT: Once the system is observed, the qubit's state collapses into one of its basis states (0,1)**
  • Quantum Superposition: Two quantum states can be added together to create another valid quantum state. **Every quantum state can be represented as a sum of two or more other distinct states**
  • Quantum Parallelism: A register of n qubits will have 2n basis states of its own. Since a register can exist in a superposition of all these states at once, it is possible to apply a computation to all 2n register states rather than just one
  • Modular Period: How often a modular set repeats. Say set = {2,4,8,16,32,64,..} ; set (mod 15) = {2,4,8,1,2,4,8,1,..} ; The period would be 4
  • Shor's Period Finding Algorithm:
    1) Create superposition of states by applying Hadamard Gates to all qubits in the input register
    2) Implement f as a quantum transform. Shor used repeated squaring
    3) Perform Quantum Fourier Transform

Shor's Pseudocode

The algorithm consists of two steps.
a) Turns the factoring into the period finding problem
b) Finds period using quantum fourier transform


Factoring a large odd integer N
1) Choose random m < N. Compute gcd(m,N) using Euclideans which runs in poly-time. If gcd(m,N) ≠ 1, we have found a non-trivial factor and are finished.
2) Use quantum computer to determine unknown period P with probability close to 1
3) If P is odd, return to step 1 ; Else, proceed. The Pr[P is odd] = (1/2)k, where k is the number of distinct prime factors of N.
4) Note: (mP/2 - 1)(mP/2 + 1) = mP - 1
If mP/2 + 1 ≡ 0 (mod N), return to step 1 ; Else, Proceed. Pr[mP/2 + 1 ≡ 0 (mod N)] < (1/2)k-1
5) Compute d = gcd(mP/2 - 1, N) with Euclidean. Since mP/2 + 1 ≠ 0 (mod N), it is shown that d is non-trivial factor of N.

Time to Break RSA

Let's start easy with N = 21 (p = 3, q = 7)
1) Random m = 4. gcd(21,4) = 1. Proceed.
2) Find Period of function f..f(a) = 4a mod 21. P = 3
3) P is odd, return to step 1

1) m = 5. gcd(21,5) = 1. Proceed.
2) Find P = 6.
3) P is even. Proceed.
4) 53 + 1 ≡ 0 (mod 21)? Yes, return to 1.

1) m = 8. (gcd(21,6) and gcd(21,7) are not 1). gcd(21,8) = 1. Proceed.
2) Q-Computer finds P = 2 with high probability
3) P is even. Proceed.
4) 81 + 1 ≡ 0 (mod 21)? No. Proceed.
5) d = gcd(81 - 1, 21) = 7

All of these steps can be coded classically, except for 2. This means that in theory, most of the implementation of Shor's can be written today. Our problem comes with finding the period of the function in a feasible amount of time. Shor himself has stated that we won't know the full implications of a quantum computer until one is actually built. Coming full circle, we see why The Mosca Theorem has huge consequences. We need to be able to write quantum-resistant algorithms and implement them into existing infrastructures by the time quantum computers are built.

The Quantum Timeline

  • 1960: Stephen Wiesner creates the idea of conjugate coding. Rejected by the science community at the time, Wiesner was years ahead of his time
  • conjugate coding: An idea of a "quantum multiplexing" channel was introduced. This allowed for one party to send two messages to another party. The receiver decides which message to read, inevitably destroying the other message
  • 1973: Holveo's theorem proved n qubits cannot carry more than n classical bits
  • 1975: Poplavskii showed that simulating quantum systems on a classical computer would be infeasible due to the superposition principle
  • 1980: Yuri Manin proposed the idea of quantum computing
  • 1984: 20 years later, Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard utilize Wiesner's conjugate coding technique for the distribution of cryptographic keys
  • 1985: David Deutsch describes the first universal quantum computer, which can simulate any other quantum computer
  • 1980: Yuri Manin proposed the idea of quantum computing
  • 1991: Artur Ekert invents entanglement based communication
  • 1993: Dan Simon invented an oracle problem that run under a quantum computer would run exponentially faster than a classic computer
  • 1994: The Shor algorithm, shown above, was introduced by Bell Labs' Peter Shor
  • 1996: Lov Grover invents the quantum database search algorithm. Having a quadratic speedup, this algorithm is applicable to brute-force search and random search algorithms
  • 1997: Papers were published recognizing gates for quantum computers based on nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)
  • 1998: First experimental demonstration of a quantum algorithm was shown. Deutsch's oracle problem was solved using a 2-qubit NMR quantum computer.
  • 2000: First working 5-qubit and 7-qubit NMR computer was built
  • 2003: DARPA quantum network becomes operational
  • 2006: University of Copenhagen develops the idea of quantum teleportation between photons and atoms
  • 2008: Possible non-entanglement quantum computing found
  • 2009: NIST r/w individual qubits and performs multiple operations on qubits
  • 2010: Qubits manipulated electrically, not magnetically
  • 2011: D-Wave claims to have developed the first commercially available quantum computer (goes for 10 mil)
  • 2014: Snowden confirms Penetrating Hard Targets project claiming the NSA seeked development into a quantum capable computer for cyrptographic purposes
  • 2014: Using quantum teleportation, scientists sent data over a distance of 10 ft with 0% error rate
  • 2014: Nick Dattani & Nathan Bryans break the record for largest number factored on a quantum device: 56153 (previous was 143)

The Future

The idea of Quantum Computing is quite a new topic in the ever-changing computing world. Every year, numerous breakthroughs are discovered, inching closer and closer to quantum standardization. It is a race against time and by the looks of it, we are losing. The closest organizations and companies to the computing world are starting to worry too.

(US) NSA: Issued a release in 2015 advising all companies not to switch over to elliptic curve algorithms (more secure than public key). They announced they were going to initiate a transition to quantum-resistant algorithms in the near future

(EU) PQCRYPTO: Offers insight into our cryptography future and offers technical packages including: 1) PQ-crypto for small devices (embedded devices), 2) PQ-crypto for the Internet (making signatures safe), and 3) PQ-crypto for the cloud (all user data)

(EU) ETSI: A Independent, non-profit organization helping anyone implement secure practices

(US) NIST: Made a report on Post-Quantum Cryptography and many other resources available to the public, free of charge

(US) IETF: A general signature system using Merkle trees. Most asymmetric authentication will be insecure when a quantum computer becomes widely available. IETF does not rely on large integer arithmatic, so it will not be affected

What I've Learned

For one, quantum computing is a highly debated topic in the community. Take the company D-Wave for example. They were the first company to launch the universal quantum computer. D-Wave has partnered with NASA, Google, and countless others in order to push quantum technology into the marketplace. Their computers use quantum annealing, which is basically a way to measure quantum fluctuations for experiments and tests. They based the prototypes off of the papers written by renowned scientist Umesh Vazirani.

In 2007, D-Wave claimed to have an operational 28-qubit quantum annealing computer, and next year claimed to have built a 128-qubit computer. Both of these claims have yet to be verified though. Then in 2015, D-Wave said they have created the first 1000+-qubit computer.

Now here is where things get interesting. Umesh Vazirani, the scientist whose papers are the basis for the D-Wave prototypes, actually rejects the notion that D-Wave built a quantum computer. His beliefs stem from the fact that quantum entanglement is completely absent from D-Wave technology. He's not alone in his opinion either.
